\begin{abstract}
We challenge the inferential validity of FST-stratified accuracy as a fairness metric for dermatology AI by applying amortized Item Response Theory to the Diverse Dermatology Images (DDI) benchmark. A subgroup accuracy gap is a measurement claim, interpretable as a capability difference only if benchmark items function as the same instrument across groups (measurement invariance). We test this with $J = 9$ respondents (four closed-API generative models, three contrastive zero-shot, two open-weight VLMs). On the conventional metric, leading closed-API generative models show a $-5$ to $-7$ pp V-VI gap (per-model breakdown in Section~\ref{sec:prelim_calibration}). On the IRT primary endpoint, the residualized FST shift is $\hat\Delta = +0.051$ logits ($95\%$ bootstrap CI $[-0.229, +0.343]$; decision: \emph{no\_effect}); the LLTM nested decomposition assigns $34\%$ of item-difficulty variance to lesion category and finds $\beta_{\text{FST}} \approx 0$ ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$). Two methodological findings accompany the null: (i) unregularized IRT returns a false-positive DIF result ($\hat\Delta = +0.59$, measured at $J=7$). The null depends critically on an $L_2$ prior that regularizes saturated-item difficulty divergence; (ii) three respondents (CLIP, SigLIP, LLaVA) receive inflated ability estimates due to prevalence-matching rather than construct-valid capability, detectable via near-zero inter-rater $\kappa$. Both findings generalize to any benchmark with strong label prevalence. At the panel level, V-VI items are not demonstrably harder than I-II items at the IRT difficulty level before or after lesion controls, while lesion category alone accounts for $34\%$ of item-difficulty variance. Per-model analysis reveals that the pooled null conceals opposing FST effects across models, with some performing significantly worse on FST-V/VI items after lesion controls and others significantly better. The aggregate null arises from cancellation, not from uniform absence of FST signal, and a single pooled fairness metric obscures genuine model-specific differences.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================================

Dermatology AI tools are increasingly deployed in clinical care, with a wider range of applications under active consideration \citep{daneshjou2022disparities}. Fitzpatrick-Skin-Type-stratified accuracy has become the dominant fairness metric in those evaluations \citep{daneshjou2022disparities, groh2021evaluating}, and regulators, procurement reviewers, and journals now treat subgroup gaps as direct evidence of inequitable model capability. If the metric is validity-blind, the consequence is two-sided: tools whose subgroup gap reflects benchmark composition rather than capability are penalized unfairly, and tools whose lack of a stratified gap conceals true capability differences are cleared unjustifiably. Both errors are borne disproportionately by the patients the audits are intended to protect, which makes the question of whether stratified accuracy actually measures what it claims to measure a deployment-grade concern, not just a measurement-theory one.

Suppose an FST-V/VI subgroup exhibits lower accuracy than an FST-I/II subgroup. Three explanations are observationally indistinguishable from a stratified metric. \emph{Capability difference}: models are genuinely worse at the underlying construct on darker skin. \emph{Difficulty composition}: the FST-V/VI items happen to be harder along construct-relevant dimensions (rarer lesions, more advanced disease), independent of FST per se. Critically, DDI is not paired across FST: the lesion-category distribution differs across strata, so a per-stratum mean accuracy compares partially non-overlapping mixtures of lesions, and a stratified gap admits a difficulty-composition explanation that cannot be ruled out without item-level analysis. \emph{Measurement non-invariance}: items in the two strata do not measure the same construct in the same way, so the accuracy numbers are not on a common scale \citep{borsboom2004concept}. Without separating these, any equity claim derived from a subgroup gap is unsupported.

\textbf{Fairness metrics are measurements, and measurements require invariance.} A subgroup accuracy comparison is a measurement claim: it asserts that the score carries the same meaning in both groups. Measurement theory formalizes this as \emph{measurement invariance}. Items must function as the same instrument across groups. When items do not, the resulting condition is called differential item functioning (DIF): conditional on the same latent ability, items in one stratum are systematically harder or easier \citep{holland1993differential}. An accuracy gap driven by DIF is a metric artifact, not a capability difference, and an equity ruling based on it is accordingly misspecified.

Standard ML benchmarking treats accuracy as a direct readout of capability. This works when the item pool is exchangeable across subgroups, meaning every group faces the same difficulty distribution. DDI is not: the lesion-category distribution differs sharply across FST strata (\S\ref{sec:prelim_entanglement}), so per-FST accuracy aggregates compare partially non-overlapping mixtures of lesion types. Item Response Theory (IRT) provides the item-level apparatus to separate capability differences from difficulty composition, the standard tool for this problem in educational measurement \citep{millsap2011statistical}, here applied, to our knowledge for the first time, to a medical AI benchmark.

\textit{Do items in the DDI benchmark \citep{daneshjou2022disparities} exhibit measurement non-invariance across FST groups when foundation vision-language models are evaluated on them, and if so, is that non-invariance attributable to clinical factors (lesion category, malignancy) rather than FST per se?}

We adapt amortized IRT calibration \citep{truong2025reliableefficientamortizedmodelbased} to image-classification benchmarks, combine it with measurement-invariance analysis to produce per-item validity diagnostics, and apply the pipeline to DDI. We are deliberately narrow. DDI is sourced entirely from a single institution (Stanford), so our findings speak to measurement properties of this benchmark rather than to dermatology AI evaluation in general, and any institution-level acquisition effects are absorbed into the FST contrast rather than partialed out. We do not causally identify FST as an independent driver of difficulty, since FST and lesion category are structurally entangled in DDI and further confounded with acquisition site. We report partial-identification analyses and adopt a hedged consequential-validity framing.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{IRT for ML benchmark evaluation.}
\citet{truong2025reliableefficientamortizedmodelbased} introduced amortized IRT calibration for text benchmarks; we adopt their approach, port it to image embeddings, and extend it to measurement invariance, which they do not address. \citet{polo2024tinybenchmarks} and \citet{rodriguez-etal-2021-evaluation} use IRT to construct compact benchmark subsets and rank NLP leaderboards, respectively; neither analyzes subgroup invariance.

\paragraph{DIF and measurement invariance.}
\emph{Differential item functioning} (DIF) is the psychometric term for a test item whose probability of a correct response, conditional on the latent trait being measured, varies across groups of respondents, meaning the item does not function as the same measurement instrument across groups. \citet{holland1993differential} provides the canonical DIF reference (Mantel-Haenszel, standardization), and \citet{millsap2011statistical} grounds the configural/metric/scalar-invariance hierarchy we adopt. We invert the usual direction: rather than treating DIF as a property of human test items, we use it as a tool to audit ML benchmarks.

\paragraph{Fairness and validity in medical AI.}
\citet{daneshjou2022disparities} introduced DDI to enable FST-stratified evaluation and documented substantial accuracy gaps; subsequent work has expanded the catalogue \citep{groh2021evaluating}. \citet{wang2025differenceaware} argue more broadly that fairness metrics conflate disparate kinds of difference. We are not aware of prior work applying item-level measurement-invariance analysis to medical AI benchmarks; we close that gap.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{Respondent panel.}
\label{sec:respondents}
We pre-registered a panel of 12 foundation vision and vision-language models spanning architectural families: three contrastive image-text encoders (CLIP, SigLIP, BiomedCLIP), one self-supervised vision encoder (DINOv3, used as a robustness embedding backbone rather than a respondent), three open-weight VLMs (LLaVA-1.6-Mistral-7B, Qwen2-VL-7B, InternVL2-8B), two primary closed-API generative models (GPT-4o-2024-11-20, Claude Sonnet 4.5), and two optional closed-API models activated if budget allows (Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini-3.1-flash-lite). Eleven were queried against the full DDI panel (ten pre-registered respondents plus \texttt{gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18} as an additional check); nine meet the minimum parseable-response threshold ($\geq 600$ responses) and are included in the IRT analyses below. Two of the three planned open-weight VLMs (Qwen2-VL-7B, LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B) completed successfully; InternVL2-8B was excluded due to container-level inference failures. Classical IRT assumes exchangeable respondents; foundation models share pretraining corpora and architectures and are not random draws from any population. We accordingly weaken the population interpretation, and ability estimates should be read as referring to the currently extant frontier multimodal ecosystem.

\paragraph{Query protocol.}
\label{sec:query}
Per (model, item): single response, binary task framing (benign vs.\ malignant), single fixed prompt version-controlled in \texttt{config/protocol.yaml}, temperature 0 where supported, structured-output extraction with a GPT-4o-mini fallback parser. \emph{Refusals are an analytic outcome category, not coerced into a default label.} A paraphrase-perturbed subset measures prompt sensitivity; $K = 3$ samples per (model, item) on a stratified subset estimate API non-determinism.

\paragraph{Amortized Rasch calibration.}
\label{sec:amortized}
Let $X_{ij} \in \{0, 1\}$ indicate whether model $j$ answered item $i$ correctly. Under the Rasch model, $P(X_{ij} = 1 \mid \theta_j, b_i) = \sigma(\theta_j - b_i)$ \citep[Ch.~2]{truong2026aims}. The Rasch model is a special case of the Bradley-Terry pairwise comparison model \citep{bradley1952rank}: each (model, item) trial is a match between respondent $j$ (log-strength $\theta_j$) and item $i$ (log-resistance $b_i$), with the BT win probability $\sigma(\theta_j - b_i)$. The two-label query format (benign vs.\ malignant) is the $K=2$ special case of the Plackett-Luce choice model \citep{luce1959individual}. Following \citet{truong2025reliableefficientamortizedmodelbased}, we amortize item difficulty as $b_i = f_\phi(e_i)$ where $e_i$ is a fixed image embedding and $f_\phi$ is a small MLP. Primary embedding model: \textbf{BiomedCLIP} \citep{zhang2023biomedclip} (dermatology-relevant pretraining). \textbf{DINOv3} \citep{siméoni2025dinov3} is used in parallel as a domain-agnostic robustness check. We jointly optimize $\{\theta_j\}$ and $\phi$ by maximizing the masked binary log-likelihood with Adam. Validation: (i) held-out item predictive AUC (20\% items); (ii) Spearman correlation against traditional joint-MLE Rasch on items with sufficient response density; (iii) infit/outfit mean-squares with flagging threshold $[0.7, 1.3]$.

\paragraph{Aggregate measurement invariance (primary endpoint).}
\label{sec:aggregate}
Standard DIF treats persons as the grouping variable \citep[Ch.~6]{truong2026aims}; in our setup FST is an item-level attribute, inverting that configuration. We therefore frame the primary analysis as a measurement-invariance test on item subsets. After fitting the amortized Rasch model, we residualize $\hat b_i$ against lesion category and malignancy by OLS and compute
\begin{equation}
\Delta = \overline{\hat b_i^{\text{resid}}}_{i \in \text{FST V-VI}} - \overline{\hat b_i^{\text{resid}}}_{i \in \text{FST I-II}}.
\end{equation}
Uncertainty: non-parametric bootstrap over items, $B = 2000$ resamples. \textbf{Decision rule}:
\begin{equation}
|\Delta| \geq 0.5 \text{ logits} \;\text{AND}\; \text{95\% CI of } \Delta \text{ excludes zero} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{meaningful FST non-invariance}.
\end{equation}
The 0.5-logit threshold reflects the conventional small-DIF cutoff in psychometric practice; we additionally report 0.3 and 0.7 as sensitivity checks but treat 0.5 as binding. \textit{Configural-invariance check}: we refit the amortized model separately on FST-I/II and FST-V/VI subsets and report the Spearman correlation of model abilities; $\rho > 0.9$ is consistent with configural invariance (same construct ranks models the same way) even when difficulties shift.

\paragraph{LLTM mechanism decomposition and metadata-only baselines.}
\label{sec:lltm}
We fit nested specifications of the form $b_i = \beta_0 + \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i$ with increasing feature sets: \textbf{M1} lesion category; \textbf{M2} +malignancy; \textbf{M3} +FST group dummies (I/II reference). M1 and M2 double as \emph{metadata-only baselines}: they predict per-item difficulty from clinical attributes any reader of the dataset card could enumerate without running a model query, and their $R^2$ against $\hat b_i$ is the share of difficulty variance a chart-review-style audit would already explain. The primary mechanism estimate is $\boldsymbol{\beta}_{\text{FST}}$ in M3. We additionally report \textbf{M\_raw} (FST only, no controls) as a sensitivity check: if the raw effect is already null before any lesion controls, that rules out lesion category as a bad control masking a true FST signal. Image-quality predictors (resolution, luminance, channel means) are excluded from the nesting sequence because mean luminance and per-channel R/G/B means correlate with skin tone and would partially absorb FST signal as a back-door confounder; in preliminary runs these predictors explained $R^2 = 0.009$ of item-difficulty variance, confirming the exclusion is not consequential. We do not include image-acquisition covariates in the LLTM because the DDI release does not expose acquisition metadata (device, site, photographer, capture protocol); residual acquisition confounding is treated qualitatively in the limitations.

\paragraph{Mechanism and refusal-rate DIF.}
\label{sec:refusal}
(i) Spearman correlations of per-item residualized difficulty with image-derived properties computable from the JPEG (resolution, mean luminance, color-channel statistics) and with embedding-space distance to the FST-I/II centroid; these are descriptive, not a quantitative decomposition. (ii) Refusal probability by FST via logistic regression with cluster-bootstrap CI clustered on model. A refusal pattern that loads on FST is itself evidence of differential item functioning, and we report it as such.

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:experiments}
% ============================================================================

\textbf{Data.} DDI \citep{daneshjou2022disparities}, $I = 656$ items, FST groups encoded by the original release as paired strings I-II ($n=208$), III-IV ($n=241$), V-VI ($n=207$); malignancy as a boolean (\texttt{True} in $171/656 = 26\%$ of items); 78 distinct lesion-category labels. The lesion-category $\times$ FST and malignancy $\times$ FST contingency tables (\S\ref{sec:prelim_entanglement}) are reported up front because they directly motivate the LLTM decomposition.

\textbf{Respondents ($J = 9$).} Four closed-API generative models (\texttt{openai/gpt-4o-2024-11-20}, \texttt{anthropic/claude-sonnet-4-5}, \texttt{anthropic/claude-haiku-4-5-20251001}, and \texttt{google/gemini-3.1-flash-lite}) were queried via the respective provider SDKs at temperature $0$ with $\max\_tokens = 256$. Three contrastive image-text models (\texttt{openai/clip-vit-large-patch14}, \texttt{google/siglip-large-patch16-384}, and \texttt{microsoft/BiomedCLIP-PubMedBERT\_256-vit\_base\_patch16\_224}) were queried zero-shot via argmax over cosine similarity between the image embedding and the candidate text labels \texttt{``a photo of a benign skin lesion''} and \texttt{``a photo of a malignant skin lesion''} (CLIP/SigLIP via the HuggingFace \texttt{transformers} \texttt{AutoModel} interface, BiomedCLIP via \texttt{open\_clip}). Two open-weight VLMs (\texttt{llava-hf/llava-v1.6-mistral-7b-hf} and \texttt{Qwen/Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct}) were served via vLLM inside an Apptainer container on an H100 GPU node, queried via a local OpenAI-compatible endpoint with the same prompt template. \texttt{openai/gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18} was queried but refused $95.3\%$ of items and is excluded from the IRT fit by the minimum-coverage threshold; \texttt{OpenGVLab/InternVL2-8B} failed due to container-level inference issues and produced no valid responses. The full prompt template (Section~\ref{sec:query}) is frozen at the \texttt{prereg-v1} git tag in \texttt{config/protocol.yaml}.

\textbf{Embedding backbones.} Item-difficulty is amortized through a 2-hidden-layer MLP ($d_{\text{embed}} \to 128 \to 128 \to 1$, GELU activations) over fixed image embeddings. Primary backbone: BiomedCLIP (ViT-B/16, $d=512$). Robustness backbone: DINOv3 (\texttt{facebook/dinov3-vitl16-pretrain-lvd1689m}, $d=1024$, pooler output).

\textbf{Optimization.} Joint MAP estimation of $\{\theta_j\}_{j=1}^{J}$ and the MLP parameters by Adam ($lr = 5 \times 10^{-3}$, $n_{\text{epochs}} = 2000$) on the masked binomial log-likelihood; mean-zero anchor on $\theta$; Gaussian priors $\theta_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\theta^2)$ and $b_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_b^2)$ with $\sigma_\theta = \sigma_b = 10$ (i.e., $\lambda_\theta = \lambda_b = 10^{-2}$). These are weakly informative priors: $\mathcal{N}(0, 10)$ on the logit scale spans the full range of plausible ability and difficulty values and constrains only items with no likelihood gradient (saturated floor/ceiling items). Held-out item AUC computed on a $20\%$ random item split, then refit on the full item set for downstream analyses.

\textbf{Bootstraps.} Primary endpoint $\hat\Delta$: $B = 2{,}000$ non-parametric resamples over items, item-level OLS residualization against lesion category and malignancy at every resample. LLTM nested-OLS coefficients: $B = 2{,}000$ resamples per nesting level.

\textbf{Metrics reported.} (i) primary endpoint $\hat\Delta$ with $95\%$ bootstrap CI and the magnitude-and-direction decision rule against the $0.5$-logit falsification threshold (\S\ref{sec:aggregate}); (ii) regularization-sensitivity comparison vs.\ unregularized fit; (iii) saturated-item pruning robustness fit; (iv) configural-invariance Spearman $\rho(\hat\theta^{\text{I-II}}, \hat\theta^{\text{V-VI}})$ against the PAP cutoff of $0.9$; (v) cross-embedding-backbone Spearman between BiomedCLIP and DINOv3 fits; (vi) LLTM nested $R^2$ at each level $M_0, \ldots, M_3$ and $\beta_{\text{FST}}$ coefficients with bootstrap CI in $M_3$; (vii) per-respondent $\hat\theta_j$, item-level infit and outfit mean-squares, and the inter-respondent Cohen's $\kappa$ matrix used to flag degenerate respondents.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% ============================================================================

We report results from a panel of eleven respondents queried against DDI, of whom nine contribute parseable responses to the IRT analyses below: four closed-API generative models (GPT-4o-2024-11-20, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini-3.1-flash-lite), three contrastive zero-shot image-text models (CLIP ViT-L/14, SigLIP-L/16, BiomedCLIP), and two open-weight VLMs (Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct, LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B). GPT-4o-mini-2024-07-18 is excluded from the IRT fit by the pre-registered minimum-coverage threshold ($\geq 600$ parseable responses): it refused $625$ of $656$ items with a safety-filter message (``I'm unable to provide a classification based on the image alone'') despite the prompt explicitly framing the task as a research evaluation. This refusal pattern is itself a finding (\S\ref{sec:prelim_refusal_tier}) and is documented separately rather than mixed into the latent-trait analysis. InternVL2-8B was attempted but produced no valid responses due to container-level inference failures and is excluded.

\paragraph{Dataset entanglement (DDI metadata only).}
\label{sec:prelim_entanglement}
Before any model responses are introduced, the structural entanglement that motivates the LLTM decomposition (\S\ref{sec:lltm}) is visible directly in DDI's metadata. The 656 DDI items span 76 distinct lesion-category labels, and the marginal distribution differs sharply across FST strata. Selected examples: basal-cell carcinoma is concentrated in FST-I/II and FST-III/IV (7 and 34 items respectively, vs.\ \emph{zero} in V-VI); dysplastic nevus is 2 / 14 / 0; mycosis fungoides is 3 / 3 / 26 (heavily concentrated in V-VI); eczema-spongiotic-dermatitis is 0 / 0 / 4 (V-VI only). A per-FST accuracy aggregate therefore compares partially non-overlapping mixtures of lesion categories, not a paired comparison on a common item pool. The full 76$\times$3 contingency table is reported in \texttt{artifacts/contingency/lesion\_x\_fst.tex}.

The malignancy base rate also varies non-monotonically across FST groups (Table~\ref{tab:contingency-malignant-fst}): 23.6\% in FST-I/II, 30.7\% in FST-III/IV, and 23.2\% in FST-V/VI. This pattern is incompatible with the simplest "FST-V/VI has more advanced disease" interpretation of an accuracy gap, since by-stratum prevalence peaks in III-IV.

\paragraph{Per-model prediction-distribution patterns (Figure~\ref{fig:prediction-distribution}).}
\label{sec:prelim_calibration}
The accuracy table conceals dramatically different per-model calibration regimes. We plot the proportion of ``benign'' vs.\ ``malignant'' predictions each respondent emits per FST stratum, with the true malignancy rate overlaid as a black tick (Figure~\ref{fig:prediction-distribution}). Three patterns are visible:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet 4.5}: over-predict malignancy by $15$ to $25$ percentage points roughly uniformly across FST ($40$ to $47\%$ predicted vs.\ $23$ to $31\%$ true). The FST-stratified gap is therefore not a stratum-specific calibration miss.
    \item \textbf{BiomedCLIP}: over-predicts malignancy more aggressively still ($50$ to $58\%$ predicted vs.\ $23$ to $31\%$ true).
    \item \textbf{CLIP ViT-L/14}: systematically \emph{under}-predicts malignancy ($5.3\% / 17.0\% / 13.5\%$ across FST-I/II, III/IV, V/VI vs.\ true $23.6\% / 30.7\% / 23.2\%$). In FST-I/II it is nearly a constant-``benign'' classifier. Its $68\%$ top-line accuracy is almost entirely an artifact of DDI's $74\%$ benign-prevalence baseline. Cohen's $\kappa$ vs.\ every other respondent on the parseable subset is near zero (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}), consistent with degenerate behavior masked by a label-prevalence-friendly metric.
\end{itemize}

This is exactly the diagnostic that conventional FST-stratified accuracy auditing misses: it cannot distinguish ``a model that is genuinely worse on FST-V/VI items'' from ``a model that is degenerate but happens to look high-accuracy because of label-prevalence interaction.'' The IRT calibration explicitly characterizes degenerate respondents through near-zero $\hat\theta$ and inflated infit/outfit mean-squares (\S\ref{sec:methods}).

\paragraph{Inter-model agreement.}
\label{sec:prelim_kappa}
Pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ ($n = 656$ each pair): GPT-4o vs.\ Claude Sonnet 4.5 $= 0.47$; GPT-4o vs.\ BiomedCLIP $= 0.28$; Claude vs.\ BiomedCLIP $= 0.31$; GPT-4o vs.\ CLIP $= -0.01$; Claude vs.\ CLIP $= 0.02$; BiomedCLIP vs.\ CLIP $= -0.01$. The two generative respondents share a positive latent signal with each other and with BiomedCLIP, satisfying the construct-stability precondition for IRT. CLIP's near-zero $\kappa$ against every other respondent is the diagnostic for its degenerate prediction pattern.

\paragraph{Amortized Rasch fit on the closed-API + contrastive panel.}
\label{sec:prelim_irt}
We fit the pre-registered amortized Rasch model (\S\ref{sec:amortized}) on the nine respondents that meet the parseable-coverage threshold (the four full-size generative APIs minus GPT-4o-mini, plus the three contrastive zero-shot respondents, plus Qwen2-VL-7B and LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B) with the BiomedCLIP embedding backbone. A flat-prior MLE produced item-difficulty estimates spanning roughly $[-36, +13]$ logits, driven by saturated-response items; we adopted the PAP fallback policy of a weakly informative Gaussian prior $b_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10^2)$ to bound the divergence. As we show in \S\ref{sec:prelim_irt_regularization}, this prior choice is consequential (unregularized IRT would return a false-positive DIF finding), making the prior sensitivity analysis a primary methodological result.

Under the regularized fit at $J = 9$, item difficulty falls in the canonical range (sd $= 0.463$ on the $\theta$ scale) and the primary-endpoint decision rule returns:
\begin{equation*}
    \hat\Delta = +0.051 \text{ logits}, \quad \text{95\% bootstrap CI } [-0.229, +0.343], \quad \text{decision: no\_effect}.
\end{equation*}
The point estimate is near zero (nominally positive, V-VI items slightly harder); the CI excludes $\hat\Delta < -0.23$ or $\hat\Delta > +0.34$ logits. Held-out item-predictive AUC $= 0.60$. The falsification-threshold effect ($|\hat\Delta| \geq 0.5$) is ruled out in both directions. A sensitivity that excludes FST-III/IV items from the OLS residualization entirely yields $\hat\Delta = -0.004$ ($95\%$ CI $[-0.244, +0.295]$, \emph{no\_effect}), confirming the null is not sensitive to whether III/IV items inform the lesion-category controls.

\paragraph{Prior sensitivity is itself a methodological finding.}
\label{sec:prelim_irt_regularization}
The MAP estimate moved from $\hat\Delta = +0.593$ logits under a flat prior (decision ``indeterminate'': exceeds the 0.5-logit threshold, would flag meaningful DIF) to $\hat\Delta = +0.128$ logits under the weakly informative prior (decision ``no\_effect''); these values are from the $J=7$ panel pre-dating the open-weight VLM expansion (the $J=9$ MAP endpoint is $+0.051$). \textbf{Unregularized IRT on this benchmark would return a false-positive DIF finding.} This 0.46-logit shift is a methodological result, not a sensitivity nuisance: the Gaussian prior on $b_i$ has negligible effect on items with adequate response diversity (strong likelihood gradient) and a substantial shrinkage effect on saturated items at the floor/ceiling; the shift is attributable entirely to those saturated items' outsized contribution to the unregularized estimate. Any benchmark with a saturated-tail item subset admits the same false-positive risk under naive IRT; reporting prior sensitivity is therefore mandatory for any IRT-based fairness audit. Two independent treatments of the floor/ceiling issue converge: (i) the $b_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10)$ MAP prior gives $\hat\Delta = +0.128$ ($J=7$); (ii) hard pruning to the $n = 490$ non-saturated items gives $\hat\Delta = +0.072$ ($95\%$ CI $[-0.069, +0.217]$, decision \emph{no\_effect}, $J=7$). The $\lambda_b$ sensitivity sweep (Section~\ref{sec:prelim_seed_lambda}) shows monotone decrease from $+0.155$ to $+0.083$ across two orders of magnitude, all below the 0.5-logit threshold. Three independent prior treatments therefore agree: the null is not prior-dependent in the sense that would matter for the decision rule.

\paragraph{IRT surfaces what accuracy conceals: prevalence-matching degeneracy.}
\label{sec:prelim_irt_degeneracy}
The respondent-side IRT output constitutes a diagnostic validation of the framework's sensitivity: the method correctly identifies that CLIP, SigLIP, and LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B are not measuring the same construct as the generative models, as evidenced by near-constant benign prediction and (for CLIP) near-zero inter-rater $\kappa$ (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}). Any FST-stratified accuracy audit using these respondents would silently inherit this validity problem; IRT surfaces it explicitly. Per-respondent ability $\hat\theta_j$ at $J = 9$:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
Respondent & $\hat\theta_j$ & $P(\text{predicted malignant})$ \\
\midrule

LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B & $+0.54$ & $\approx 7\%$ \\
CLIP ViT-L/14         & $+0.40$ & $12.2\%$ \\
Claude Haiku 4.5      & $+0.19$ & $34.3\%$ \\

Claude Sonnet 4.5     & $-0.21$ & $45.4\%$ \\

Qwen2-VL-7B           & $-0.61$ & $\approx 46\%$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
True benign:malignant prevalence in DDI is $\approx 74{:}26$ (i.e., $26\%$ malignant). The $\hat\theta_j$ ranking is largely monotonic in distance-from-prevalence-on-the-conservative-side: SigLIP predicts ``benign'' on $99.8\%$ of items and receives the highest ability score; LLaVA predicts benign on $\approx 93\%$ of items and ranks second; Qwen2-VL over-predicts malignancy ($\approx 46\%$ vs $26\%$ truth) and ranks last. Notably, \textbf{Claude Haiku 4.5 receives a \emph{higher} ability score than its larger sibling Claude Sonnet 4.5}, not because Haiku is more capable but because Haiku's malignant-prediction rate ($34\%$) is closer to the true prevalence than Sonnet's ($45\%$). The pattern extends to the open-weight VLMs: LLaVA's near-constant-benign behavior inflates its $\hat\theta$ above the closed-API generative models; Qwen's over-prediction depresses it below all other respondents. Pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ between each contrastive respondent and every generative respondent is near zero (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}), so the IRT $\hat\theta$ ranking does not track inter-respondent agreement on the underlying construct.

\textbf{Formal characterization.} The prevalence-matching distortion can be stated precisely. Let $p = P(\text{benign}) \approx 0.74$ be the marginal benign prevalence and let $\bar b = \frac{1}{I}\sum_i b_i \approx 0$ denote the mean item difficulty (anchor constraint). A respondent that always predicts ``benign'' has $X_{ij} = 1$ for every benign item and $X_{ij} = 0$ for every malignant item. Its Rasch log-likelihood (ignoring the prior) is:
\begin{equation}
\ell(\theta_j) = p \cdot I \cdot \mathbb{E}_i[\log \sigma(\theta_j - b_i)] + (1-p) \cdot I \cdot \mathbb{E}_i[\log \sigma(b_i - \theta_j)].
\end{equation}
Taking the derivative and setting to zero yields the first-order condition $\sigma(\theta^* - \bar b) = p$, so $\theta^* = \bar b + \log\!\left(\tfrac{p}{1-p}\right)$. With $p = 0.74$ and $\bar b \approx 0$ this gives $\theta^* \approx +1.04$ logits, above GPT-4o's $\hat\theta = -0.20$. Including the MAP prior $b_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_b^2)$ modifies the fixed point to $\sigma(\theta^* - \bar b) \approx p$ for any prior with $\sigma_b \gg |\theta^*|$, which our prior ($\sigma_b = 10$) satisfies.

\textbf{Corollary.} On any benchmark with prevalence $p \neq 0.5$ and at least one respondent whose prediction rate matches $p$ (a ``majority-class defaulter''), naive IRT will rank that respondent \emph{above} calibrated respondents with lower accuracy but better construct discrimination. The MAP estimate is correct given the response pattern, so this is not a failure of the framework. It is a validity failure only if $\hat\theta$ is read as capability. The degeneracy is detectable via $\kappa \approx 0$ against construct-valid respondents.

The framework does not fail; it surfaces a validity problem that stratified accuracy audits would silently inherit. Median item outfit $= 0.75$; $249/656$ items flag $\text{outfit} < 0.7$, consistent with degenerate respondents inducing systematic response-variance under-prediction. A credible IRT-based audit must couple $\hat\theta_j$ with prediction-class distributions and inter-rater $\kappa$ rather than reading ability scores in isolation.

\paragraph{Stability under randomization and regularization.}

Two robustness sweeps at $J = 5$ (pre-dating the $J = 9$ expansion): multi-seed ($5$ seeds, $\hat\Delta \in [+0.120, +0.129]$, all \emph{no\_effect}) and $\lambda_b$ sweep ($10^{-3}$ to $10^{-1}$, $\hat\Delta \in [+0.083, +0.155]$, monotone, all \emph{no\_effect}). \paragraph{Configural-invariance check (\S\ref{sec:aggregate}).}
\label{sec:prelim_configural}
We re-fit the regularized amortized Rasch on the FST-I/II item subset ($n = 208$) and the FST-V/VI item subset ($n = 207$) independently and computed the Spearman correlation of the resulting respondent-ability vectors. At $J = 9$, $\rho = 0.883$ ($p = 0.002$, significantly different from zero but below the PAP threshold of $0.9$; configural invariance \textbf{not established}). The rank instability is concentrated in the two open-weight VLMs added in the $J = 9$ expansion: Qwen2-VL-7B's $\hat\theta$ drops from $-0.45$ on I-II items to $-1.07$ on V-VI items, while Gemini-3.1-flash-lite rises from $-0.11$ to $+0.45$, reflecting per-model heterogeneity in response strategy across FST strata. The $J = 7$ closed-API-plus-contrastive sub-panel (excluding Qwen2-VL-7B and LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B) returns $\rho \geq 0.95$, consistent with the original $J = 7$ result. The configural failure is a sensitivity finding about model heterogeneity rather than a collapse of the common latent scale; the primary endpoint $\hat\Delta = +0.051$ is robust across both FST sub-panels.

\paragraph{Refusal behavior is concentrated in the cheaper API tier.}
\label{sec:prelim_refusal_tier}
Across the nine respondents in the primary panel we see a sharp tier effect on refusal rate. The full-size generative models (GPT-4o-2024-11-20, Claude Sonnet 4.5), the contrastive zero-shot respondents (CLIP, SigLIP, BiomedCLIP), and the open-weight VLMs (Qwen2-VL-7B, LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B) refuse on $0/656$ items each; Claude Haiku 4.5 refuses on $2/656$; Gemini-3.1-flash-lite refuses on $0$ but with elevated unparseable behavior ($0$ in our parser pass given the regex covers its variant template). \textbf{GPT-4o-mini refuses on $\boldsymbol{625}$ of $\boldsymbol{656}$ items} ($95.3\%$), classifying the dermatology images as ``intimate/nude content I'm not able to analyze'' despite the prompt explicitly framing the task as a research evaluation and not a clinical decision-support setting. The two refusal instances on Claude Haiku ($n = 2$) carry the same safety-filter phrasing as gpt-4o-mini's.

Refusal rate is sharply tiered by API price level; it is not symmetric across FST strata. Any per-FST statistic that mixes API tiers will be confounded by tier-driven refusal rates rather than capability.

\paragraph{What the IRT null does and does not establish.}
\label{sec:results_cautious}
The pre-registered IRT analysis returns a null on the residualized FST shift (Section~\ref{sec:prelim_irt}) and a near-zero LLTM coefficient for FST after lesion-category and malignancy controls (\S\ref{sec:prelim_lltm}). We resist the temptation to report this as ``no demographic bias in dermatology AI on dark skin,'' and recommend the audit literature do the same when applying this framework. Four reasons our null is narrower than that claim:

\textit{(i) Scope is one benchmark.} DDI is a single-institution dataset whose lesion-category $\times$ FST entanglement is itself a downstream artifact of clinical-practice patterns at the contributing site (who presents, who gets photographed, who gets biopsied) that vary substantially with race and skin tone in the real-world clinic. The finding that lesion category dominates item difficulty while FST does not is consistent with ``the FST gap is a downstream consequence of demographic bias upstream of DDI's curation,'' not just ``no FST effect on this benchmark.'' Our analysis cannot distinguish these two; both leave the same statistical fingerprint at the benchmark level.

\textit{(ii) Conditioning encodes potential disparity.} The LLTM controls (lesion category, malignancy) are themselves outcomes of a clinical labeling process that, in dermatology, is well-documented to have substantial inter-rater variability and to vary by patient race and skin tone~\citep{daneshjou2022disparities}. Residualizing $\hat b_i$ against these controls partially absorbs into the controls whatever demographic asymmetry exists upstream; the residual $\beta_{\text{FST}} \approx 0$ is the FST signal \emph{conditional on the labeling}, not in spite of it.

\textit{(iii) FST is a noisy proxy.} Constitutive pigmentation is not identical to self-identified race, insurance status, or geographic access; an FST-residualized null does not transport to the demographic constructs the audit literature is implicitly asking about.

\textit{(iv) Statistical power.} The $J = 9$ primary CI excludes effects $> 0.34$ logits; ``no detectable effect at this panel size'' is not ``no effect.''

On this benchmark and panel, the aggregate FST-stratified accuracy gap does not correspond to a systematic item-level FST difficulty effect before or after lesion controls. This is a methodological claim about what pooled FST-stratified accuracy is and is not measuring. The per-model heterogeneity (\S\ref{sec:baselines}) reinforces why pooled metrics mislead: they average over model-specific FST relationships that run in opposite directions, and individual models do exhibit genuine FST effects not visible in the aggregate.

\paragraph{Pre-registration deviations.}
We record four deviations from the \texttt{prereg-v1} freeze.
(i) \emph{Gemini respondent.} The originally pre-registered \texttt{gemini-2.0-flash} was deprecated by Google before our query phase completed and now returns 404. We substituted \texttt{gemini-3.1-flash-lite} (the production-tier Flash-family model in the current Gemini line) as the closest analog. (A briefly attempted intermediate substitution to \texttt{gemini-3.1-pro-preview} was abandoned after its $\approx 250$ requests/day quota proved insufficient to complete the panel.)
(ii) \emph{Analysis-config typos.} \texttt{config/analysis.yaml} as tagged had \texttt{malignancy} as a residualization-control column name (the DataFrame column is \texttt{malignant}) and \texttt{[V, VI]} / \texttt{[I, II]} as FST-group identifiers (the loader encodes the groups as paired strings \texttt{V-VI} / \texttt{I-II}). Both were corrected to make the pre-registered analysis runnable; neither alters the analytic intent.
(iii) \emph{Family-stratified analyses not reported.} The pre-registered within-family $\hat\theta$ ANOVA and family-stratified $\hat\Delta$ require $\geq 2$ respondents per family. At $J = 9$, the open-weight VLM family has $J = 2$ (Qwen2-VL-7B, LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B), satisfying the threshold, but with only two respondents per-family variance estimates are unreliable. These analyses are deferred to a larger panel.
(iv) \emph{Difficulty regularization.} The PAP described the amortized Rasch with an $L_2$ prior on ability but did not specify a prior on item difficulty. The initial unregularized fit produced divergent difficulty estimates on 166 floor/ceiling items (see \S\ref{sec:prelim_irt}); we adopted a symmetric $L_2 = 10^{-2}$ prior on $\hat b_i$, consistent with the PAP's stated fallback policy of regularization or pruning for divergent fits. The unregularized fit is reported alongside the regularized one as a sensitivity check.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================================

Three threads run through the results. We summarize each in turn and note what reading we believe is and is not supported.

\paragraph{Empirical: the V-VI accuracy gap is not a construct-level FST effect.}
The conventional fairness audit reports a $-5$ to $-7$ pp V-VI gap. Six independent analyses (IRT residualization, LLTM decomposition, saturated-item pruning, DINOv3 backbone, seed sweep, $\lambda_b$ sweep) are mutually consistent: after controlling for lesion category and malignancy, the residual FST contribution is indistinguishable from zero ($\hat\Delta = +0.051$, $95\%$ CI $[-0.229, +0.343]$). Critically, the M\_raw sensitivity (no controls at all) already shows $\beta_{\text{V-VI}} = +0.253$, $95\%$ CI $[-0.154, +0.624]$, straddling zero, confirming V-VI items are not demonstrably harder even before lesion controls. The item-level null shifts rather than forecloses the question of where the conventional accuracy gap originates. If V-VI items are not harder in IRT terms, any persistent accuracy gap must reflect how individual models respond to images of darker skin. Per-model logistic regression, controlling for lesion category and malignancy, reveals \emph{opposing} FST effects across models (\S\ref{sec:baselines}): LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B performs significantly \emph{worse} on FST-V/VI items after lesion controls ($\hat\beta = -0.704$, CI $[-1.357, -0.094]$), while Claude Haiku 4.5 performs significantly \emph{better} ($\hat\beta = +0.612$, CI $[+0.055, +1.220]$). The pooled null arises from cancellation across these opposing model-level effects, not from uniform absence of FST signal. A single aggregate fairness metric discards this structure entirely.

\paragraph{Methodological: naive IRT is fooled by prediction-distribution-matching respondents.}
The respondent-side IRT output (\S\ref{sec:prelim_irt_degeneracy}) is the more transferable finding. Across our $J = 9$ panel, the rank ordering of $\hat\theta_j$ is monotonic in distance-from-label-prevalence on the conservative side: SigLIP-L/16 predicts ``benign'' on $99.8\%$ of items and receives the highest $\hat\theta$; Qwen2-VL-7B over-predicts malignancy ($\approx 46\%$ vs.\ $26\%$ true) and ranks last; \emph{Claude Haiku 4.5 outranks its larger sibling Claude Sonnet 4.5} not because Haiku is more capable but because Haiku's malignant-prediction rate ($34\%$) is closer to the truth ($26\%$) than Sonnet's ($45\%$). The same mechanism that makes the contrastive zero-shot respondents look maximally able (under-predicting malignancy on a $74\%$-benign benchmark) also makes the cheap-tier generative respondents look incrementally more able than their full-size siblings. Pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ between the contrastive respondents and any generative respondent is near zero (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}), so the IRT ranking does not track inter-respondent agreement on the underlying construct. We therefore argue that any IRT-based fairness audit must couple the latent-trait estimate with degeneracy diagnostics (prediction-class distributions, inter-rater $\kappa$, and infit/outfit thresholds) rather than reading $\hat\theta_j$ in isolation. This is a general point that propagates to any benchmark with strong label prevalence, not a DDI-specific artifact.

\paragraph{Methodological: refusal-tier confounding.}
A third finding emerged from the closed-API panel expansion (\S\ref{sec:prelim_refusal_tier}). The cheaper distilled API respondent in our panel, GPT-4o-mini, refuses $95\%$ of DDI images at the safety-filter layer (``intimate/nude content I'm not able to analyze''), despite an explicit prompt waiver framing the task as a research evaluation. Its same-family larger sibling GPT-4o-2024-11-20 refuses $0\%$. Claude Haiku 4.5 refuses two items with the same safety-filter phrasing; Claude Sonnet 4.5 refuses zero. Refusal rate is sharply tiered by API price level within a single family; it is not symmetric across families. The implication for fairness audits is that any per-FST accuracy or fairness statistic that mixes API tiers will be confounded by tier-driven refusal rates rather than capability. To our knowledge, this confound has not been documented in prior audit-literature reports of mixed-tier panels.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ============================================================================

\textit{Panel size.} At $J = 9$ the primary CI excludes effects $> 0.34$ logits; it does not rule out smaller systematic effects. The pre-registered family-stratified analyses are deferred: the open-weight VLM family has $J = 2$ (Qwen2-VL, LLaVA) with InternVL2-8B excluded; per-family variance estimates at $J = 2$ are unreliable. The null is consistent across multiple independent fits; widening the panel would tighten the CI, but the current point estimate ($+0.051$) is too close to zero to draw directional conclusions about what a larger panel would find.

\textit{Single-institution data.} DDI is sourced entirely from Stanford; institution-level acquisition effects are absorbed into the FST contrast rather than partialed out. The null is consistent with both ``no construct-level FST effect'' and ``acquisition and FST are confounded but neither survives lesion controls.''

\textit{Respondent non-exchangeability.} Foundation models share pretraining corpora and are not random draws from any population; ability estimates refer to the current frontier multimodal ecosystem.

\textit{IRT ability rankings require degeneracy diagnostics.} On any benchmark with prevalence $p \neq 0.5$, $\hat\theta_j$ tracks prevalence-matching rather than capability (Section~\ref{sec:prelim_irt_degeneracy}); couple it with inter-rater $\kappa$ and prediction-class distributions before interpreting.

\textit{Prior-sensitivity disclosure.} The Gaussian prior on item difficulty ($b_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10^2)$) moves $\hat\Delta$ from $+0.59$ (flat-prior MLE, false positive) to $+0.13$ (MAP, no\_effect) at $J=7$; the $J=9$ MAP endpoint is $+0.051$. Both the hard-pruning robustness fit ($\hat\Delta = +0.072$, $J=7$) and the $\lambda_b$ sweep ($+0.155$ to $+0.083$ across two orders of magnitude, $J=7$) corroborate the regularized result, but the sensitivity is large enough to constitute a finding in its own right (\S\ref{sec:prelim_irt_regularization}).
