\begin{abstract}
We evaluate the inferential validity of Fitzpatrick skin type (FST)-stratified accuracy as a fairness metric for dermatology AI by applying amortized Item Response Theory (IRT) to the Diverse Dermatology Images (DDI) benchmark. We investigate subgroup accuracy gap as a measurement claim, and examine whether benchmark items present measurement invariance across groups. We test this with $J = 9$ respondents (four closed-API generative models, three contrastive zero-shot, two open-weight VLMs). Exploring the residualized FST shift through IRT, we find $\hat\Delta = +0.051$ logits ($95\%$ bootstrap CI $[-0.229, +0.343]$). The LLTM nested decomposition assigns $34\%$ of item-difficulty variance to lesion category and finds $\beta_{\text{FST}} \approx 0$ ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$). This null result highlights a key methodological caveat, that some models look artificially capable under IRT. Three respondents (CLIP, SigLIP, LLaVA) receive inflated ability estimates due to prevalence-matching and this is broadly applicable to any benchmark with strong label prevalence. At the panel level, V-VI items are not demonstrably harder than I-II items at the IRT difficulty level before or after lesion controls. Per-model analysis reveals that the pooled null conceals opposing FST effects across models, with some performing significantly worse on FST-V/VI items after lesion controls and others significantly better. For example, per-model logistic regression finds that LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B performs significantly worse on V-VI items after lesion controls ($\hat\beta = -0.704$, CI $[-1.357, -0.094]$) while Claude Haiku 4.5 performs significantly better ($\hat\beta = +0.612$, CI $[+0.055, +1.220]$). This implies that the aggregate null arises from cancellation of these opposing effects, not from uniform absence of FST signal. 
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================================

Dermatology AI tools are increasingly deployed in clinical care, with a wider range of applications under active consideration \citep{daneshjou2022disparities}. Fitzpatrick-Skin-Type-stratified accuracy has become the dominant fairness metric in those evaluations \citep{daneshjou2022disparities, groh2021evaluating}, and regulators, procurement reviewers, and journals now treat subgroup gaps as direct evidence of inequitable model capability. If the metric is validity-blind, tools whose subgroup gap reflects benchmark composition rather than capability are penalized unfairly, and tools whose lack of a stratified gap conceals true capability differences are cleared unjustifiably. Both errors are borne disproportionately by the patients the audits are intended to protect, which makes the question of whether stratified accuracy actually measures what it claims to measure a deployment-grade concern, not just a measurement-theory one.

Suppose an FST-V/VI subgroup exhibits lower accuracy than an FST-I/II subgroup. A stratified metric fails to distinguish three key cases: (1) capability difference, when models are genuinely worse at the underlying construct on darker skin, (2) difficulty composition, when the FST-V/VI items happen to be harder along construct-relevant dimensions, and (3) measurement non-invariance, where items in the two strata do not measure the same construct in the same way, so the accuracy numbers are not on a common scale \citep{borsboom2004concept}. Without separating these, equity claims derived from a subgroup gap are hard to support.

This is particularly important as fairness metrics are measurements, and measurements require invariance. A subgroup accuracy comparison is a measurement claim that asserts that the score carries the same meaning in both groups. Measurement theory formalizes this as measurement invariance, where items must function as the same instrument across groups. When items do not, the resulting condition is called differential item functioning (DIF). This means that conditioned on the same latent ability, items in one stratum are systematically harder or easier \citep{holland1993differential}. An accuracy gap driven by DIF is a metric artifact, not a capability difference, and an equity ruling based on it is accordingly misspecified.

Standard ML benchmarking treats accuracy as a direct readout of capability. This works when the item pool is exchangeable across subgroups, meaning every group faces the same difficulty distribution. However, this is generally not true, especially for medicine, where dataset composition can be constrained by non-random factors. Specifically for DDI, it is known that the lesion-category distribution differs sharply across FST strata (\S\ref{sec:prelim_entanglement}), so per-FST accuracy aggregates compare partially non-overlapping mixtures of lesion types. Item Response Theory (IRT) provides the item-level apparatus to separate capability differences from difficulty composition, the standard tool for this problem in educational measurement \citep{millsap2011statistical}, here applied, to our knowledge for the first time, to a medical AI benchmark.

Our central question is: \textit{do items in the DDI benchmark \citep{daneshjou2022disparities} exhibit measurement non-invariance across FST groups when foundation vision-language models are evaluated on them, and if so, is that non-invariance attributable to clinical factors (lesion category, malignancy) rather than FST ?}

We adapt amortized IRT calibration \citep{truong2025reliableefficientamortizedmodelbased} to image-classification benchmarks, combine it with measurement-invariance analysis to produce per-item validity diagnostics, and apply the pipeline to DDI.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{IRT for ML benchmark evaluation.}
\citet{truong2025reliableefficientamortizedmodelbased} introduced amortized IRT calibration for text benchmarks. Here, we adopt their approach, port it to image embeddings, and extend it to measurement invariance, which they do not address. \citet{polo2024tinybenchmarks} and \citet{rodriguez-etal-2021-evaluation} use IRT to construct compact benchmark subsets and rank NLP leaderboards, respectively. We extend it to analysis of subgroup invariance.

\paragraph{DIF and measurement invariance.}
DIF \citet{holland1993differential} is the psychometric term for a test item whose probability of a correct response, conditional on the latent trait being measured, varies across groups of respondents, meaning the item does not function as the same measurement instrument across groups.  \citet{millsap2011statistical} grounds the configural/metric/scalar-invariance hierarchy we adopt. We invert the usual direction, and instead of treating DIF as a property of human test items, we use it as a tool to audit ML benchmarks.

\paragraph{Fairness and validity in medical AI.}
\citet{daneshjou2022disparities} introduced DDI to enable FST-stratified evaluation and documented substantial accuracy gaps. Subsequent work that has expanded the catalog \citep{groh2021evaluating}. \citet{wang2025differenceaware} argue more broadly that fairness metrics conflate disparate kinds of difference.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{Respondent panel.}
\label{sec:respondents}
We pre-registered a panel of 11 foundation vision and vision-language models spanning architectural families: three contrastive image-text encoders (CLIP, SigLIP, BiomedCLIP), three open-weight VLMs (LLaVA-1.6-Mistral-7B, Qwen2-VL-7B, InternVL2-8B), two primary closed-API generative models (GPT-4o-2024-11-20, Claude Sonnet 4.5), and two additional closed-API models (Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini-3.1-flash-lite).  Additionally, we registered one self-supervised vision encoder (DINOv3) for use as a robustness embedding backbone rather than a respondent. These 11 models were queried against the full DDI panel. Nine meet the minimum parseable-response threshold ($\geq 600$ responses) and are included in the IRT analyses below. Of note, classical IRT assumes exchangeable respondents. Since foundation models share pretraining corpora and architectures and are not random draws from any population, we accordingly weaken the population interpretation, and ability estimates should be read as referring to the currently extant frontier multimodal ecosystem.

\paragraph{Query protocol.}
\label{sec:query}
For every model, item pair, we query for a single response to a binary classification task (benign vs.\ malignant). There is a single fixed prompt version-controlled in \texttt{config/protocol.yaml}, with a temperature set to 0 where supported. We use structured-output extraction, with a GPT-4o-mini fallback parser. Refusals are treated as an analytic outcome category, and not coerced into a default label. % A paraphrase-perturbed subset measures prompt sensitivity. To estimate API non-determinism, we sampled for $K = 3$ samples per (model, item) on a stratified subset.

\paragraph{Amortized Rasch calibration.}
\label{sec:amortized}
Let $X_{ij} \in \{0, 1\}$ indicate whether model $j$ answered item $i$ correctly. Under the Rasch model, $P(X_{ij} = 1 \mid \theta_j, b_i) = \sigma(\theta_j - b_i)$ \citep[Ch.~2]{truong2026aims}. % The Rasch model is a special case of the Bradley-Terry pairwise comparison model \citep{bradley1952rank}: each (model, item) trial is a match between respondent $j$ (log-strength $\theta_j$) and item $i$ (log-resistance $b_i$), with the BT win probability $\sigma(\theta_j - b_i)$. The two-label query format (benign vs.\ malignant) is the $K=2$ special case of the Plackett-Luce choice model \citep{luce1959individual}. 
Following \citet{truong2025reliableefficientamortizedmodelbased}, we amortize item difficulty as $b_i = f_\phi(e_i)$ where $e_i$ is a fixed image embedding and $f_\phi$ is a small MLP. The primary embedding model we used was BiomedCLIP \citep{zhang2023biomedclip} with DINOv3 \citep{siméoni2025dinov3} used in parallel as a domain-agnostic robustness check. We jointly optimize $\{\theta_j\}$ and $\phi$ by maximizing the masked binary log-likelihood with Adam. We flag items with infit/outfit mean-squares outside \$[0.7, 1.3]\$ as potentially misfitting. 

\paragraph{Aggregate measurement invariance (primary endpoint).}
\label{sec:aggregate}
Standard DIF treats persons as the grouping variable \citep[Ch.~6]{truong2026aims}. In our setup, FST is an item-level attribute, and we invert that configuration. We therefore frame the primary analysis as a measurement-invariance test on item subsets. After fitting the amortized Rasch model, we residualize $\hat b_i$ against lesion category and malignancy by OLS and compute
\begin{equation}
\Delta = \overline{\hat b_i^{\text{resid}}}_{i \in \text{FST V-VI}} - \overline{\hat b_i^{\text{resid}}}_{i \in \text{FST I-II}}.
\end{equation}
To get a confidence interval, we resample the items (with replacement) 2000 times and recompute $\Delta$ each time. We use the following decision rule to determine whether it is a meaningful FST effect:
\begin{equation}
|\Delta| \geq 0.5 \text{ logits} \;\text{AND}\; \text{95\% CI of } \Delta \text{ excludes zero} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{meaningful FST non-invariance}.
\end{equation}
The 0.5-logit threshold reflects the conventional small-DIF cutoff in psychometric practice. We additionally report 0.3 and 0.7 as sensitivity checks. To test for configural-invariance check, we refit the amortized model separately on FST-I/II and FST-V/VI subsets and report the Spearman correlation of model abilities; $\rho > 0.9$ is consistent with configural invariance (same construct ranks models the same way) even when difficulties shift.

\paragraph{LLTM mechanism decomposition and metadata-only baselines.}
\label{sec:lltm}
We fit nested specifications of the form $b_i = \beta_0 + \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i$ with increasing feature sets: \textbf{M1} lesion category; \textbf{M2} +malignancy; \textbf{M3} +FST group dummies (I/II reference). The primary mechanism estimate is $\boldsymbol{\beta}_{\text{FST}}$ in M3. We additionally report \textbf{M\_raw} (FST only, no controls) as a sensitivity check. If the raw effect is already null before any lesion controls, that rules out lesion category as a bad control masking a true FST signal. Image-quality predictors (resolution, luminance, channel means) are excluded from the nesting sequence because mean luminance and per-channel R/G/B means correlate with skin tone and would partially absorb FST signal as a back-door confounder (in preliminary runs these predictors explained $R^2 = 0.009$ of item-difficulty variance, confirming the exclusion is not consequential).

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% ============================================================================

We report results from a panel of nine parseable respondants to the IRT analyses below: four closed-API generative models (GPT-4o-2024-11-20, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini-3.1-flash-lite), three contrastive zero-shot image-text models (CLIP ViT-L/14, SigLIP-L/16, BiomedCLIP), and two open-weight VLMs (Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct, LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B). GPT-4o-mini-2024-07-18 is excluded from the IRT fit by the pre-registered minimum-coverage threshold ($\geq 600$ parseable responses) as it refused $625$ of $656$ items with a safety-filter message (``I'm unable to provide a classification based on the image alone'') despite the prompt explicitly framing the task as a research evaluation. 
\label{sec:prelim_entanglement}
Before any model responses are introduced, the structural entanglement that motivates the LLTM decomposition (\S\ref{sec:lltm}) is visible directly in DDI's metadata. The 656 DDI items span 78 distinct lesion-category labels, and the marginal distribution differs sharply across FST strata. For example, basal-cell carcinoma is concentrated in FST-I/II and FST-III/IV (7 and 34 items respectively, vs.\ \emph{zero} in V-VI); dysplastic nevus is 2 / 14 / 0; mycosis fungoides is 3 / 3 / 26 (heavily concentrated in V-VI); eczema-spongiotic-dermatitis is 0 / 0 / 4 (V-VI only). A per-FST accuracy aggregate therefore compares partially non-overlapping mixtures of lesion categories, not a paired comparison on a common item pool. \paragraph{Per-model prediction-distribution patterns (Figure~\ref{fig:prediction-distribution}).}
\label{sec:prelim_calibration}
The accuracy table conceals dramatically different per-model calibration regimes. We plot the proportion of ``benign'' vs.\ ``malignant'' predictions each respondent emits per FST stratum, with the true malignancy rate overlaid as a black tick (Figure~\ref{fig:prediction-distribution}). There are six distinct calibration patterns:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet 4.5}: over-predict malignancy by $15$ to $25$ percentage points roughly uniformly across FST ($40$ to $47\%$ predicted vs.\ $23$ to $31\%$ true).
    \item \textbf{Gemini-3.1-flash-lite}: the largest FST-dependent over-prediction of any closed-API model: $\approx\!66\%$ predicted malignant in FST I-II, declining to $\approx\!52\%$ in FST V-VI, both far above the $23$--$31\%$ true rate.
    \item \textbf{Claude Haiku 4.5}: predicted-malignant rate decreases across FST strata ($\approx\!39\%$ in I-II to $\approx\!29\%$ in V-VI), keeping it closer to the true prevalence than the other closed-API models.
    \item \textbf{BiomedCLIP and Qwen2-VL-7B}: aggressive over-prediction throughout ($50$--$60\%$ predicted vs.\ $23$--$31\%$ true).
    \item \textbf{CLIP ViT-L/14}: systematically \emph{under}-predicts malignancy ($5.3\% / 17.0\% / 13.5\%$ across FST I-II, III-IV, V-VI vs.\ true $23.6\% / 30.7\% / 23.2\%$). In FST I-II it is nearly a constant-``benign'' classifier. Its $69\%$ top-line accuracy is almost entirely an artifact of DDI's $74\%$ benign-prevalence baseline. Cohen's $\kappa$ vs.\ every other respondent is near zero (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}), consistent with degenerate behavior masked by label-prevalence.
    \item \textbf{SigLIP-L/16 and LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B}: SigLIP predicts ``benign'' on $99.8\%$ of items; LLaVA moderately under-predicts malignancy ($\approx\!7\%$ predicted vs.\ $26\%$ true). Both have $\kappa \approx 0$ against every generative respondent (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}).

\paragraph{Inter-model agreement.}
\label{sec:prelim_kappa}
The full $9 \times 9$ pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ matrix reveals three agreement clusters. \emph{Generative cluster} (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini): pairwise $\kappa = 0.34$--$0.56$. \emph{Open-weight VLMs}: Qwen2-VL-7B agrees moderately with the generative cluster ($\kappa = 0.35$--$0.47$); LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B is lower ($\kappa = 0.23$--$0.37$). BiomedCLIP lies between the two: $\kappa = 0.28$--$0.34$ with generative respondents. \emph{Degenerate classifiers}: CLIP ViT-L/14 and SigLIP-L/16 have $\kappa \approx 0$ against every other respondent, confirming that their high raw accuracy reflects majority-class exploitation rather than shared diagnostic signal.

\paragraph{Amortized Rasch fit.}
\label{sec:prelim_irt}
We fit the pre-registered amortized Rasch model (\S\ref{sec:amortized}) on the nine respondents that meet the parseable-coverage threshold with the BiomedCLIP embedding backbone. A flat-prior MLE produced item-difficulty estimates spanning roughly $[-36, +13]$ logits on the 105 saturated-pattern items (all-correct or all-wrong across the panel); we adopted the PAP fallback policy of a weakly informative Gaussian prior $b_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10^2)$ to bound the divergence. The flat-prior estimate ($\hat\Delta = +0.097$, $n = 551$ pruned non-saturated items), the hard-pruning estimate ($+0.096$), and a $\lambda_b$ sweep ($\hat\Delta \in [+0.049, +0.062]$ across two orders of magnitude) all agree: the \emph{no\_effect} decision is not prior-dependent.

Under the regularized fit at $J = 9$, item difficulty falls in the canonical range (sd $= 0.463$ on the $\theta$ scale) and the primary-endpoint decision rule returns:
\begin{equation*}
    \hat\Delta = +0.051 \text{ logits}, \quad \text{95\% bootstrap CI } [-0.229, +0.343].
\end{equation*}
The point estimate is near zero, though nominally positive, with V-VI items slightly harder. The CI excludes $\hat\Delta < -0.23$ or $\hat\Delta > +0.34$ logits. The falsification-threshold effect ($|\hat\Delta| \geq 0.5$) is ruled out in both directions. % A sensitivity that excludes FST-III/IV items from the OLS residualization entirely yields $\hat\Delta = -0.004$ ($95\%$ CI $[-0.244, +0.295]$, \emph{no\_effect}), confirming the null is not sensitive to whether III/IV items inform the lesion-category controls.

\paragraph{IRT surfaces prevalence-matching degeneracy.}
\label{sec:prelim_irt_degeneracy}
The respondent-side IRT output constitutes a diagnostic validation of the framework's sensitivity. It correctly identifies that CLIP, SigLIP, and LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B are not measuring the same construct as the generative models, as evidenced by near-constant benign prediction and (for CLIP) near-zero inter-rater $\kappa$ (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}). Per-respondent ability $\hat\theta_j$ at $J = 9$:

True benign:malignant prevalence in DDI is $\approx 74{:}26$ (i.e., $26\%$ malignant). The $\hat\theta_j$ ranking is largely monotonic in distance-from-prevalence-on-the-conservative-side: SigLIP predicts ``benign'' on $99.8\%$ of items and receives the highest ability score; LLaVA predicts benign on $\approx 93\%$ of items and ranks second; Qwen2-VL over-predicts malignancy ($\approx 46\%$ vs $26\%$ truth) and ranks last. Notably, \textbf{Claude Haiku 4.5 receives a \emph{higher} ability score than its larger sibling Claude Sonnet 4.5}, not because Haiku is more capable but because Haiku's malignant-prediction rate ($34\%$) is closer to the true prevalence than Sonnet's ($45\%$). The pattern extends to the open-weight VLMs: LLaVA's near-constant-benign behavior inflates its $\hat\theta$ above the closed-API generative models. Qwen's over-prediction depresses it below all other respondents. Pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ between each contrastive respondent and every generative respondent is near zero (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}), so the IRT $\hat\theta$ ranking does not track inter-respondent agreement on the underlying construct.

 The prevalence-matching distortion can be characterized formally. Let $p = P(\text{benign}) \approx 0.74$ be the marginal benign prevalence and let $\bar b = \frac{1}{I}\sum_i b_i \approx 0$ denote the mean item difficulty (anchor constraint). A respondent that always predicts ``benign'' has $X_{ij} = 1$ for every benign item and $X_{ij} = 0$ for every malignant item. Its Rasch log-likelihood (ignoring the prior) is:
\begin{equation}
\ell(\theta_j) = p \cdot I \cdot \mathbb{E}_i[\log \sigma(\theta_j - b_i)] + (1-p) \cdot I \cdot \mathbb{E}_i[\log \sigma(b_i - \theta_j)].
\end{equation}
Taking the derivative and setting to zero yields the first-order condition $\sigma(\theta^* - \bar b) = p$, so $\theta^* = \bar b + \log\!\left(\tfrac{p}{1-p}\right)$. With $p = 0.74$ and $\bar b \approx 0$ this gives $\theta^* \approx +1.04$ logits, above GPT-4o's $\hat\theta = -0.20$. Including the MAP prior $b_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_b^2)$ modifies the fixed point to $\sigma(\theta^* - \bar b) \approx p$ for any prior with $\sigma_b \gg |\theta^*|$, which our prior ($\sigma_b = 10$) satisfies.

The corollary establishes that on DDI's 74\%-benign data, any model that always predicts "benign" achieves 74\% raw accuracy and IRT ability $\hat\theta \approx +1.05$, above calibrated models that predict both classes but get fewer items right overall. IRT correctly infers that answering 74\% of items correctly implies high latent ability, but this is a problem, as 74\% accuracy on this benchmark reflects majority-class exploitation, not diagnostic discrimination. For a prevalence-matching respondent, IRT surfaces this problem as for these respondents, $\kappa \approx 0$ against models making genuine class distinctions, because its responses carry no information about which items are difficult. We therefore screen respondents by inter-rater $\kappa$ and exclude degenerate models from the primary $\hat\Delta$ estimate.

\end{center}
We find three key observations. First, \textbf{lesion category alone explains $34\%$ of the item-difficulty variance} (M1 $R^2 = 0.340$), while malignancy and FST together add less than $0.1\%$. Second, \textbf{the raw FST effect is null}: M\_raw, which regresses difficulty on FST with no controls at all, yields $\beta_{\text{V-VI}} = +0.253$ $[-0.154, +0.624]$ and the CI straddles zero. This rules out lesion category as a bad control masking a true FST signal: V-VI items are not demonstrably harder even before conditioning. Third (the pre-registered primary endpoint), \textbf{adding FST to the full design matrix (M3) produces $\Delta R^2 < 0.001$ and a coefficient indistinguishable from zero}: $\beta_{\text{V-VI}} = +0.063$ $[-0.303, +0.458]$, CI straddling zero in both directions. The sign is nominally positive (V-VI items marginally harder after controls), consistent with the residualized shift $\hat\Delta = +0.051$.

\paragraph{Composition-aware baselines without a latent-trait model.}
\label{sec:baselines}
We present two non-IRT baselines to contextualize our findings. \textbf{(i) Propensity-matched accuracy gap.} We restrict to the 28 lesion categories present in \emph{both} FST-I/II and V-VI subsets ($n = 189$ I-II items, $n = 170$ V-VI items). The matched accuracy gap is $-0.046$ pp ($95\%$ CI $[-0.092, -0.001]$, which barely excludes zero); the unmatched gap on all I-II vs.\ V-VI items is $-0.033$ pp. \textbf{(ii) Pooled logistic regression.} Fitting $\text{logit}\,P(X_{ij}=1) \sim \text{FST} + \text{lesion\_category} + \text{malignant} + \text{model\_id}$ on the full response matrix yields $\hat\beta_{\text{FST(V-VI)}} = +0.034$ ($95\%$ CI $[-0.257, +0.308]$), consistent with $\hat\Delta \approx 0$.

\paragraph{Stability under randomization and regularization.}
\label{sec:prelim_seed_lambda}
Two robustness sweeps at $J = 9$: multi-seed ($5$ seeds, $\hat\Delta \in [+0.051, +0.059]$, all \emph{no\_effect}) and $\lambda_b$ sweep ($10^{-3}$ to $10^{-1}$, $\hat\Delta \in [+0.049, +0.062]$, all \emph{no\_effect}). The sweeps confirm the null is not seed- or $\lambda_b$-dependent.

\paragraph{Configural-invariance check (\S\ref{sec:aggregate}).}
\label{sec:prelim_configural}
We re-fit the regularized amortized Rasch on the FST-I/II item subset ($n = 208$) and the FST-V/VI item subset ($n = 207$) independently and computed the Spearman correlation of the resulting respondent-ability vectors. At $J = 9$, $\rho = 0.883$ ($p = 0.002$, significantly different from zero but below the PAP threshold of $0.9$; configural invariance \textbf{not established}). The rank instability is concentrated in the two open-weight VLMs added in the $J = 9$ expansion: Qwen2-VL-7B's $\hat\theta$ drops from $-0.45$ on I-II items to $-1.07$ on V-VI items, while Gemini-3.1-flash-lite rises from $-0.11$ to $+0.45$, reflecting per-model heterogeneity in response strategy across FST strata. Excluding the two open-weight VLMs (Qwen2-VL-7B and LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B), the remaining seven respondents return $\rho \geq 0.95$; the rank instability is attributable to the open-weight VLMs alone. The configural failure is a sensitivity finding about model heterogeneity rather than a collapse of the common latent scale; the primary endpoint $\hat\Delta = +0.051$ is robust across both FST sub-panels.



\paragraph{Pre-registration deviations.}
We record four deviations from the \texttt{prereg-v1} freeze.
(i) \emph{Gemini respondent.} The originally pre-registered \texttt{gemini-2.0-flash} was deprecated by Google before our query phase completed and now returns 404. We substituted \texttt{gemini-3.1-flash-lite} (the production-tier Flash-family model in the current Gemini line) as the closest analog. (A briefly attempted intermediate substitution to \texttt{gemini-3.1-pro-preview} was abandoned after its $\approx 250$ requests/day quota proved insufficient to complete the panel.)
(ii) \emph{Analysis-config typos.} \texttt{config/analysis.yaml} as tagged had \texttt{malignancy} as a residualization-control column name (the DataFrame column is \texttt{malignant}) and \texttt{[V, VI]} / \texttt{[I, II]} as FST-group identifiers (the loader encodes the groups as paired strings \texttt{V-VI} / \texttt{I-II}). Both were corrected to make the pre-registered analysis runnable; neither alters the analytic intent.
(iii) \emph{Family-stratified analyses not reported.} The pre-registered within-family $\hat\theta$ ANOVA and family-stratified $\hat\Delta$ require $\geq 2$ respondents per family. At $J = 9$, the open-weight VLM family has $J = 2$ (Qwen2-VL-7B, LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B), satisfying the threshold, but with only two respondents per-family variance estimates are unreliable. These analyses are deferred to a larger panel.
(iv) \emph{Difficulty regularization.} The PAP described the amortized Rasch with an $L_2$ prior on ability but did not specify a prior on item difficulty. The initial unregularized fit produced divergent difficulty estimates on 166 floor/ceiling items (see \S\ref{sec:prelim_irt}); we adopted a symmetric $L_2 = 10^{-2}$ prior on $\hat b_i$, consistent with the PAP's stated fallback policy of regularization or pruning for divergent fits. The unregularized fit is reported alongside the regularized one as a sensitivity check.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{The V-VI accuracy gap is not a construct-level FST effect.}
Across IRT residualization, LLTM decomposition, saturated-item pruning, backbone abalation, seed sweeps, and $\lambda_b$ sweeps, we find that after controlling for lesion category and malignancy, the residual FST contribution is indistinguishable from zero. Critically, even with no controls at all, LLTM decomposition already shows $\beta_{\text{V-VI}} = +0.253$, $95\%$ CI $[-0.154, +0.624]$, confirming V-VI items are not demonstrably harder even before lesion controls. The item-level null shifts rather than forecloses the question of where the conventional accuracy gap originates. If V-VI items are not harder in IRT terms, any persistent accuracy gap must reflect how individual models respond to images of darker skin. Per-model logistic regression, controlling for lesion category and malignancy, reveals \emph{opposing} FST effects across models (\S\ref{sec:baselines}). LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B performs significantly \emph{worse} on FST-V/VI items after lesion controls ($\hat\beta = -0.704$, CI $[-1.357, -0.094]$), while Claude Haiku 4.5 performs significantly \emph{better} ($\hat\beta = +0.612$, CI $[+0.055, +1.220]$). 

\paragraph{Naive IRT is fooled by prediction-distribution-matching respondents.}
Across our $J = 9$ panel, the rank ordering of $\hat\theta_j$ is monotonic in distance-from-label-prevalence on the conservative side: SigLIP-L/16 predicts ``benign'' on $99.8\%$ of items and receives the highest $\hat\theta$; Qwen2-VL-7B over-predicts malignancy ($\approx 46\%$ vs.\ $26\%$ true) and ranks last; \emph{Claude Haiku 4.5 outranks its larger sibling Claude Sonnet 4.5} not because Haiku is more capable but because Haiku's malignant-prediction rate ($34\%$) is closer to the truth ($26\%$) than Sonnet's ($45\%$). The same mechanism that makes the contrastive zero-shot respondents look maximally able (under-predicting malignancy on a $74\%$-benign benchmark) also makes the cheap-tier generative respondents look incrementally more able than their full-size siblings. Pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ between the contrastive respondents and any generative respondent is near zero (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}), so the IRT ranking does not track inter-respondent agreement on the underlying construct. We therefore argue that any IRT-based fairness audit must couple the latent-trait estimate with degeneracy diagnostics (prediction-class distributions, inter-rater $\kappa$, and infit/outfit thresholds) rather than reading $\hat\theta_j$ in isolation. This is a general point that propagates to any benchmark with strong label prevalence.



% ============================================================================
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ============================================================================

At $J = 9$ there is limited statistical power in our tests. The pre-registered family-stratified analyses are also deferred, as per-family variance estimates at $J = 2$ are unreliable.  DDI is sourced entirely from Stanford, so institution-level acquisition effects are absorbed into the FST contrast rather than partialed out. Foundation models share pretraining corpora and are not random draws from any population, so ability estimates refer to the current frontier multimodal ecosystem.
