\begin{abstract}
We evaluate the inferential validity of Fitzpatrick skin type (FST)-stratified accuracy as a fairness metric for dermatology AI by applying amortized Item Response Theory (IRT) to the Diverse Dermatology Images (DDI) benchmark. We investigate subgroup accuracy gap as a measurement claim, and examine whether benchmark items present measurement invariance across groups. We test this with $J = 9$ respondents (four closed-API generative models, three contrastive zero-shot, two open-weight VLMs). Exploring the residualized FST shift through IRT, we find $\hat\Delta = +0.051$ logits ($95\%$ bootstrap CI $[-0.229, +0.343]$). The LLTM nested decomposition assigns $34\%$ of item-difficulty variance to lesion category and finds $\beta_{\text{FST}} \approx 0$ ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$). This null result highlights a key methodological caveat, that some models look artificially capable under IRT. Three respondents (CLIP, SigLIP, LLaVA) receive inflated ability estimates due to prevalence-matching and this is broadly applicable to any benchmark with strong label prevalence. At the panel level, V-VI items are not demonstrably harder than I-II items at the IRT difficulty level before or after lesion controls. Per-model analysis reveals opposing FST effects that cancel in the aggregate, implying that fairness audits based on panel-level summary statistics miss genuine within-model FST signal. Code is available at \url{https://github.com/sonnetx/derm-dif}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================================

Dermatology AI tools are increasingly deployed in clinical care, with a wider range of applications under active consideration \citep{daneshjou2022disparities}. Fitzpatrick-Skin-Type-stratified accuracy has become the dominant fairness metric in those evaluations \citep{daneshjou2022disparities, groh2021evaluating}, and regulators, procurement reviewers, and journals now treat subgroup gaps as direct evidence of inequitable model capability. If the metric is validity-blind, tools whose subgroup gap reflects benchmark composition rather than capability are penalized unfairly, and tools whose lack of a stratified gap conceals true capability differences are cleared unjustifiably. Both errors are borne disproportionately by the patients the audits are intended to protect, which makes the question of whether stratified accuracy actually measures what it claims to measure a deployment-grade concern, not just a measurement-theory one.

Suppose an FST-V/VI subgroup exhibits lower accuracy than an FST-I/II subgroup. A stratified metric fails to distinguish three key cases: (1) capability difference, when models are genuinely worse at the underlying construct on darker skin, (2) difficulty composition, when the FST-V/VI items happen to be harder along construct-relevant dimensions, and (3) measurement non-invariance, where items in the two strata do not measure the same construct in the same way, so the accuracy numbers are not on a common scale \citep{borsboom2004concept}. Without separating these, equity claims derived from a subgroup gap are hard to support.

Measurement theory formalizes subgroup accuracy as a measurement claim: items must function as the same instrument across groups. When they do not, the condition is called differential item functioning (DIF): items in one stratum are systematically harder or easier conditional on latent ability \citep{holland1993differential}. An accuracy gap driven by DIF is a metric artifact, not a capability difference.

Standard ML benchmarking treats accuracy as a direct readout of capability. This works when the item pool is exchangeable across subgroups, meaning every group faces the same difficulty distribution. However, this is generally not true, especially for medicine, where dataset composition can be constrained by non-random factors. Specifically for DDI, it is known that the lesion-category distribution differs sharply across FST strata (\S\ref{sec:prelim_entanglement}), so per-FST accuracy aggregates compare partially non-overlapping mixtures of lesion types. Item Response Theory (IRT) provides the item-level apparatus to separate capability differences from difficulty composition, the standard tool for this problem in educational measurement \citep{millsap2011statistical}, here applied, to our knowledge for the first time, to a medical AI benchmark.

Our central question is: \textit{do items in the DDI benchmark \citep{daneshjou2022disparities} exhibit measurement non-invariance across FST groups when foundation vision-language models are evaluated on them, and if so, is that non-invariance attributable to clinical factors (lesion category, malignancy) rather than FST ?}

We adapt amortized IRT calibration \citep{truong2025reliableefficientamortizedmodelbased} to image-classification benchmarks, combine it with measurement-invariance analysis to produce per-item validity diagnostics, and apply the pipeline to DDI.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{IRT for ML benchmark evaluation.}
\citet{truong2025reliableefficientamortizedmodelbased} introduced amortized IRT calibration for text benchmarks. Here, we adopt their approach, port it to image embeddings, and extend it to measurement invariance, which they do not address. \citet{polo2024tinybenchmarks} and \citet{rodriguez-etal-2021-evaluation} use IRT to construct compact benchmark subsets and rank NLP leaderboards, respectively. We extend it to analysis of subgroup invariance.

\paragraph{DIF and measurement invariance.}
\citet{millsap2011statistical} grounds the configural/metric/scalar-invariance hierarchy we adopt. We invert the usual direction: rather than treating DIF as a property of human test items, we use it to audit ML benchmarks.

\paragraph{Fairness and validity in medical AI.}
\citet{daneshjou2022disparities} introduced DDI to enable FST-stratified evaluation and documented substantial accuracy gaps. Subsequent work that has expanded the catalog \citep{groh2021evaluating}. \citet{wang2025differenceaware} argue more broadly that fairness metrics conflate disparate kinds of difference.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{Respondent panel.}
\label{sec:respondents}
We pre-registered a panel of 11 foundation vision and vision-language models spanning architectural families: three contrastive image-text encoders (CLIP, SigLIP, BiomedCLIP), three open-weight VLMs (LLaVA-1.6-Mistral-7B, Qwen2-VL-7B, InternVL2-8B), two primary closed-API generative models (GPT-4o-2024-11-20, Claude Sonnet 4.5), and two additional closed-API models (Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini-3.1-flash-lite).  Additionally, we registered one self-supervised vision encoder (DINOv3) for use as a robustness embedding backbone rather than a respondent. These 11 models were queried against the full DDI panel. Nine meet the minimum parseable-response threshold ($\geq 600$ responses) and are included in the IRT analyses below. 

\paragraph{Query protocol.}
\label{sec:query}
For every model, item pair, we query for a single response to a binary classification task (benign vs.\ malignant). There is a single fixed prompt, with a temperature set to 0 where supported. We use structured-output extraction, with a GPT-4o-mini fallback parser. % Refusals are treated as an analytic outcome category, and not coerced into a default label. % A paraphrase-perturbed subset measures prompt sensitivity. To estimate API non-determinism, we sampled for $K = 3$ samples per (model, item) on a stratified subset.

\paragraph{Amortized Rasch calibration.}
\label{sec:amortized}
Let $X_{ij} \in \{0, 1\}$ indicate whether model $j$ answered item $i$ correctly. Under the Rasch model, $P(X_{ij} = 1 \mid \theta_j, b_i) = \sigma(\theta_j - b_i)$ \citep[Ch.~2]{truong2026aims}. The Rasch model is a special case of the Bradley-Terry pairwise comparison model \citep{bradley1952rank}: each (model, item) trial is a match between respondent $j$ (log-strength $\theta_j$) and item $i$ (log-resistance $b_i$), with the BT win probability $\sigma(\theta_j - b_i)$. The two-label query format (benign vs.\ malignant) is also the $K=2$ special case of the Plackett-Luce choice model \citep{luce1959individual}. The Rasch model's equal-discrimination assumption (all items share a common slope) is justified here because every respondent receives the same binary prompt with identical wording; a 2PL extension would add a per-item slope parameter that is not identified without prompt variation. DDI's binary correct/incorrect format supports the unidimensionality assumption; we use configural invariance as an empirical check (\S\ref{sec:aggregate}).
Following \citet{truong2025reliableefficientamortizedmodelbased}, we amortize item difficulty as $b_i = f_\phi(e_i)$ where $e_i$ is a fixed image embedding and $f_\phi$ is a small MLP. The primary embedding model we used was BiomedCLIP \citep{zhang2023biomedclip} with DINOv3 \citep{siméoni2025dinov3} used in parallel as a domain-agnostic robustness check. We jointly optimize $\{\theta_j\}$ and $\phi$ by maximizing the masked binary log-likelihood with Adam. We flag items with infit/outfit mean-squares outside $[0.7, 1.3]$ as potentially misfitting. 

\paragraph{Aggregate measurement invariance.}
\label{sec:aggregate}
Standard DIF treats persons as the grouping variable \citep[Ch.~6]{truong2026aims}. In our setup, FST is an item-level attribute, and we invert that configuration. We therefore frame the primary analysis as a measurement-invariance test on item subsets. After fitting the amortized Rasch model, we residualize $\hat b_i$ against lesion category and malignancy by OLS and compute
\begin{equation}
\Delta = \overline{\hat b_i^{\text{resid}}}_{i \in \text{FST V-VI}} - \overline{\hat b_i^{\text{resid}}}_{i \in \text{FST I-II}}.
\end{equation}
To get a confidence interval, we resample the items (with replacement) 2000 times and recompute $\Delta$ each time. We use the following decision rule to determine whether it is a meaningful FST effect:
\begin{equation}
|\Delta| \geq 0.5 \text{ logits} \;\text{AND}\; \text{95\% CI of } \Delta \text{ excludes zero} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{meaningful FST non-invariance}.
\end{equation}
The 0.5-logit threshold reflects the conventional small-DIF cutoff in psychometric practice. We additionally report 0.3 and 0.7 as sensitivity checks. To test for configural-invariance check, we refit the amortized model separately on FST-I/II and FST-V/VI subsets and report the Spearman correlation of model abilities; $\rho > 0.9$ is consistent with configural invariance (same construct ranks models the same way) even when difficulties shift.

\paragraph{LLTM mechanism decomposition and metadata-only baselines.}
\label{sec:lltm}
The LLTM nested specification is formally equivalent to a Q-matrix analysis \citep{tatsuoka1983rule} in which item attributes (lesion category, malignancy, FST) serve as the Q-matrix columns and $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ are the attribute weights; we adopt the regression framing for interpretability. We fit nested specifications of the form $b_i = \beta_0 + \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i$ with increasing feature sets: \textbf{M1} lesion category; \textbf{M2} +malignancy; \textbf{M3} +FST group dummies (I/II reference). The primary mechanism estimate is $\boldsymbol{\beta}_{\text{FST}}$ in M3. We additionally report \textbf{M\_raw} (FST only, no controls) as a sensitivity check. If the raw effect is already null before any lesion controls, that rules out lesion category as a bad control masking a true FST signal. Image-quality predictors (resolution, luminance, channel means) are excluded from the nesting sequence because mean luminance and per-channel R/G/B means correlate with skin tone and would partially absorb FST signal as a back-door confounder (in preliminary runs these predictors explained $R^2 = 0.009$ of item-difficulty variance, confirming the exclusion is not consequential).

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% ============================================================================

We report results from a panel of nine parseable respondents to the IRT analyses below: four closed-API generative models (GPT-4o-2024-11-20, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini-3.1-flash-lite), three contrastive zero-shot image-text models (CLIP ViT-L/14, SigLIP-L/16, BiomedCLIP), and two open-weight VLMs (Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct, LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B). GPT-4o-mini-2024-07-18 is excluded from the IRT fit by the pre-registered minimum-coverage threshold ($\geq 600$ parseable responses) as it refused $625$ of $656$ items with a safety-filter message (``I'm unable to provide a classification based on the image alone'') despite the prompt explicitly framing the task as a research evaluation. 
\label{sec:prelim_entanglement}
Before any model responses are introduced, the structural entanglement that motivates the LLTM decomposition (\S\ref{sec:lltm}) is visible directly in DDI's metadata. The 656 DDI items span 78 distinct lesion-category labels, and the marginal distribution differs sharply across FST strata. For example, basal-cell carcinoma is concentrated in FST-I/II and FST-III/IV (7 and 34 items respectively, vs.\ \emph{zero} in V-VI); dysplastic nevus is 2 / 14 / 0; mycosis fungoides is 3 / 3 / 26 (heavily concentrated in V-VI); eczema-spongiotic-dermatitis is 0 / 0 / 4 (V-VI only). A per-FST accuracy aggregate therefore compares partially non-overlapping mixtures of lesion categories, not a paired comparison on a common item pool. The malignancy base rate also varies slightly across FST groups (Table~\ref{tab:contingency-malignant-fst}): 23.6\% in FST-I/II, 30.7\% in FST-III/IV, and 23.2\% in FST-V/VI.

GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet 4.5 over-predict malignancy by $15$--$25$ percentage points roughly uniformly across FST ($40$--$47\%$ predicted vs.\ $23$--$31\%$ true). \paragraph{Inter-model agreement.}
\label{sec:prelim_kappa}
The full $9 \times 9$ pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ matrix reveals three agreement clusters. \emph{Generative cluster} (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini): pairwise $\kappa = 0.34$--$0.56$. \emph{Open-weight VLMs}: Qwen2-VL-7B agrees moderately with the generative cluster ($\kappa = 0.35$--$0.47$); LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B is lower ($\kappa = 0.23$--$0.37$). BiomedCLIP lies between the two: $\kappa = 0.28$--$0.34$ with generative respondents. \emph{Degenerate classifiers}: CLIP ViT-L/14 and SigLIP-L/16 have $\kappa \approx 0$ against every other respondent, confirming that their high raw accuracy reflects majority-class exploitation rather than shared diagnostic signal.

\paragraph{Amortized Rasch fit.}
\label{sec:prelim_irt}
We fit the pre-registered amortized Rasch model (\S\ref{sec:amortized}) on the nine respondents that meet the parseable-coverage threshold with the BiomedCLIP embedding backbone. A flat-prior MLE produced item-difficulty estimates spanning roughly $[-36, +13]$ logits on the 105 saturated-pattern items (all-correct or all-wrong across the panel). We adopted the PAP fallback policy of a weakly informative Gaussian prior $b_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10^2)$ to bound the divergence. The flat-prior estimate ($\hat\Delta = +0.097$, $n = 551$ pruned non-saturated items), the hard-pruning estimate ($+0.096$), and a $\lambda_b$ sweep ($\hat\Delta \in [+0.049, +0.062]$ across two orders of magnitude) all returned the no-effect decision.

Under the regularized fit at $J = 9$, item difficulty falls in the canonical range (sd $= 0.463$ on the $\theta$ scale) and we find that $\hat\Delta = +0.051$ logits, $95\%$ bootstrap CI $[-0.229, +0.343]$. The point estimate is near zero, though nominally positive, with V-VI items slightly harder. The CI excludes $\hat\Delta < -0.23$ or $\hat\Delta > +0.34$ logits. The falsification-threshold effect ($|\hat\Delta| \geq 0.5$) is ruled out in both directions. % A sensitivity that excludes FST-III/IV items from the OLS residualization entirely yields $\hat\Delta = -0.004$ ($95\%$ CI $[-0.244, +0.295]$, \emph{no\_effect}), confirming the null is not sensitive to whether III/IV items inform the lesion-category controls.

\paragraph{IRT surfaces prevalence-matching degeneracy.}
\label{sec:prelim_irt_degeneracy}
The respondent-side IRT output constitutes a diagnostic validation of the framework's sensitivity. It correctly identifies that CLIP, SigLIP, and LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B are not measuring the same construct as the generative models, as evidenced by near-constant benign prediction and (for CLIP) near-zero inter-rater $\kappa$ (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}). Per-respondent ability $\hat\theta_j$ at $J = 9$:

True benign:malignant prevalence in DDI is $\approx 74{:}26$ (i.e., $26\%$ malignant). The $\hat\theta_j$ ranking is largely monotonic in distance-from-prevalence-on-the-conservative-side: SigLIP predicts ``benign'' on $99.8\%$ of items and receives the highest ability score; LLaVA predicts benign on $\approx 93\%$ of items and ranks second; Qwen2-VL over-predicts malignancy ($\approx 46\%$ vs $26\%$ truth) and ranks last. Notably, Claude Haiku 4.5 receives a \emph{higher} ability score than its larger sibling Claude Sonnet 4.5, not because Haiku is more capable but because Haiku's malignant-prediction rate ($34\%$) is closer to the true prevalence than Sonnet's ($45\%$). The pattern extends to the open-weight VLMs: LLaVA's near-constant-benign behavior inflates its $\hat\theta$ above the closed-API generative models. Qwen's over-prediction depresses it below all other respondents. Pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ between each contrastive respondent and every generative respondent is near zero (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}), so the IRT $\hat\theta$ ranking does not track inter-respondent agreement on the underlying construct.

 The prevalence-matching distortion can be characterized formally. Let $p = P(\text{benign}) \approx 0.74$ be the marginal benign prevalence and let $\bar b = \frac{1}{I}\sum_i b_i \approx 0$ denote the mean item difficulty (anchor constraint). A respondent that always predicts ``benign'' has $X_{ij} = 1$ for every benign item and $X_{ij} = 0$ for every malignant item. Its Rasch log-likelihood (ignoring the prior) is:
\begin{equation}
\ell(\theta_j) = p \cdot I \cdot \mathbb{E}_i[\log \sigma(\theta_j - b_i)] + (1-p) \cdot I \cdot \mathbb{E}_i[\log \sigma(b_i - \theta_j)].
\end{equation}
Taking the derivative and setting to zero yields the first-order condition $\sigma(\theta^* - \bar b) = p$, so $\theta^* = \bar b + \log\!\left(\tfrac{p}{1-p}\right)$. With $p = 0.74$ and $\bar b \approx 0$ this gives $\theta^* \approx +1.04$ logits, above GPT-4o's $\hat\theta = -0.20$. Including the MAP prior $b_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_b^2)$ modifies the fixed point to $\sigma(\theta^* - \bar b) \approx p$ for any prior with $\sigma_b \gg |\theta^*|$, which our prior ($\sigma_b = 10$) satisfies.

On DDI's $74\%$-benign data, a constant-benign classifier achieves $74\%$ raw accuracy and $\hat\theta \approx +1.05$ logits, above calibrated models that predict both classes. We therefore screen respondents by inter-rater $\kappa$ and exclude degenerate models from the primary $\hat\Delta$ estimate.

\label{sec:prelim_lltm}

Lesion category alone explains $34\%$ of the item-difficulty variance (M1 $R^2 = 0.340$), while malignancy and FST together add less than $0.1\%$. The pre-registered primary endpoint (M3) yields $\beta_{\text{V-VI}} = +0.063$, CI $[-0.303, +0.458]$, also straddling zero, with $\Delta R^2 < 0.001$. The nominally positive sign is consistent with the residualized shift $\hat\Delta = +0.051$.

\paragraph{Composition-aware baselines without a latent-trait model.}
\label{sec:baselines}
We present two non-IRT baselines to contextualize our findings. Restricting to the 28 lesion categories present in both FST-I/II and V-VI subsets ($n = 189$ I-II items, $n = 170$ V-VI items), the propensity-matched accuracy gap is $-0.046$ pp ($95\%$ CI $[-0.092, -0.001]$, barely excluding zero); the unmatched gap is $-0.033$ pp. A pooled logistic regression ($\text{logit}\,P(X_{ij}=1) \sim \text{FST} + \text{lesion\_category} + \text{malignant} + \text{model\_id}$) yields $\hat\beta_{\text{FST(V-VI)}} = +0.034$ ($95\%$ CI $[-0.257, +0.308]$), consistent with $\hat\Delta \approx 0$.

\paragraph{Per-model heterogeneity.}

\paragraph{Robustness checks.}
\label{sec:prelim_robustness}
\label{sec:prelim_seed_lambda}
\label{sec:prelim_cross_backbone}
\label{sec:prelim_configural}
Five sweeps confirm the null is not an artifact of any single modelling choice. Multi-seed ($5$ seeds, $\hat\Delta \in [+0.051, +0.059]$) and $\lambda_b$ sweeps ($10^{-3}$ to $10^{-1}$, $\hat\Delta \in [+0.049, +0.062]$) all return \emph{no\_effect}. Refitting with DINOv3 embeddings (instead of BiomedCLIP) yields an identical result: $\hat\Delta = +0.051$, CI $[-0.222, +0.340]$; per-item difficulty Spearman $\rho = 0.982$, per-respondent ability $\rho = 1.000$ across the two backbones. A generative-only fit (GPT-4o, Sonnet, Haiku, Gemini) returns $\hat\Delta = -0.209$, V-VI items nominally easier; the sign shift is consistent with degenerate respondents partially biasing $\hat\Delta$ positive, but the null conclusion is preserved. For the configural-invariance check, we refit on the FST-I/II and FST-V/VI item subsets independently; at $J = 9$, $\rho = 0.883$ (below the PAP threshold of $0.9$; configural invariance \emph{not established}). The instability traces to the two open-weight VLMs: excluding Qwen2-VL-7B and LLaVA, the remaining seven respondents return $\rho \geq 0.95$.



\paragraph{Pre-registration deviations.}
We record three deviations from the \texttt{prereg-v1} freeze. (i)~\emph{Gemini substitution}: \texttt{gemini-2.0-flash} was deprecated before our query phase completed; we substituted \texttt{gemini-3.1-flash-lite} as the closest production-tier analog. (ii)~\emph{Config-typo fixes}: two column-name mismatches in \texttt{config/analysis.yaml} were corrected; no analytic intent altered. (iii)~\emph{Difficulty regularization}: the PAP did not specify an item-difficulty prior; we adopted $L_2 = 10^{-2}$ per the PAP's stated regularization fallback for divergent fits; the unregularized fit is reported as a sensitivity check.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{The V-VI accuracy gap is not a construct-level FST effect.}
Across IRT residualization, LLTM decomposition, saturated-item pruning, backbone abalation, seed sweeps, and $\lambda_b$ sweeps, we find that after controlling for lesion category and malignancy, the residual FST contribution is indistinguishable from zero. Critically, even with no controls at all, LLTM decomposition already shows $\beta_{\text{V-VI}} = +0.253$, $95\%$ CI $[-0.154, +0.624]$, confirming V-VI items are not demonstrably harder even before lesion controls. The item-level null shifts rather than forecloses the question of where the conventional accuracy gap originates. If V-VI items are not harder in IRT terms, any persistent accuracy gap must reflect how individual models respond to images of darker skin. Per-model logistic regression, controlling for lesion category and malignancy, reveals \emph{opposing} FST effects across models (\S\ref{sec:baselines}). LLaVA-v1.6-Mistral-7B performs significantly \emph{worse} on FST-V/VI items after lesion controls ($\hat\beta = -0.704$, CI $[-1.357, -0.094]$), while Claude Haiku 4.5 performs significantly \emph{better} ($\hat\beta = +0.612$, CI $[+0.055, +1.220]$). 

\paragraph{Naive IRT is fooled by prediction-distribution-matching respondents.}
Across our $J = 9$ panel, the rank ordering of $\hat\theta_j$ is monotonic in distance-from-label-prevalence on the conservative side: SigLIP-L/16 predicts ``benign'' on $99.8\%$ of items and receives the highest $\hat\theta$; Qwen2-VL-7B over-predicts malignancy ($\approx 46\%$ vs.\ $26\%$ true) and ranks last; \emph{Claude Haiku 4.5 outranks its larger sibling Claude Sonnet 4.5} not because Haiku is more capable but because Haiku's malignant-prediction rate ($34\%$) is closer to the truth ($26\%$) than Sonnet's ($45\%$). The same mechanism that makes the contrastive zero-shot respondents look maximally able (under-predicting malignancy on a $74\%$-benign benchmark) also makes the cheap-tier generative respondents look incrementally more able than their full-size siblings. Pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ between the contrastive respondents and any generative respondent is near zero (\S\ref{sec:prelim_kappa}), so the IRT ranking does not track inter-respondent agreement on the underlying construct. We therefore argue that any IRT-based fairness audit must couple the latent-trait estimate with degeneracy diagnostics (prediction-class distributions, inter-rater $\kappa$, and infit/outfit thresholds) rather than reading $\hat\theta_j$ in isolation. This is a general point that propagates to any benchmark with strong label prevalence.

\paragraph{What worked, what did not, and what we learned.}
The IRT and LLTM pipeline worked well for its stated purpose: degeneracy diagnostics correctly flagged three non-measuring respondents before any DIF analysis, and the LLTM cleanly attributed $34\%$ of item-difficulty variance to lesion composition rather than FST. What did not work as planned was assembling the pre-registered $J = 11$ panel: one Gemini model was deprecated mid-query and GPT-4o-mini refused $95\%$ of items on safety grounds, reducing the panel to $J = 9$ and limiting power for the secondary family-stratified tests. The main lesson is that degeneracy screening must precede IRT fitting on any benchmark with strong label prevalence, and that panel composition (not just panel size) drives whether latent-trait estimates are interpretable.

% ============================================================================
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ============================================================================

At $J = 9$ there is limited statistical power in our tests. The pre-registered family-stratified analyses are also deferred, as per-family variance estimates at $J = 2$ are unreliable.  DDI is sourced entirely from Stanford, so institution-level acquisition effects are absorbed into the FST contrast rather than partialed out. Foundation models share pretraining corpora and are not random draws from any population, so ability estimates refer to the current frontier multimodal ecosystem.

% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================================

We have shown that amortized IRT combined with inter-rater $\kappa$ degeneracy screening is a principled pipeline for auditing any prevalence-unbalanced vision benchmark; without it, three respondents (CLIP, SigLIP, LLaVA) would have received inflated ability estimates and corrupted the DIF analysis. Applied to DDI, the item-level audit finds that FST-V/VI items are not demonstrably harder than FST-I/II items after lesion-category controls ($\hat\Delta = +0.051$, CI $[-0.229, +0.343]$); $34\%$ of item-difficulty variance traces to lesion composition alone, leaving less than $0.1\%$ for FST. Crucially, the aggregate null arises from cancellation of opposing per-model effects: LLaVA performs significantly \emph{worse} on FST-V/VI items after lesion controls, while Claude Haiku performs significantly \emph{better}. A single panel-level fairness metric discards this structure entirely. Future work should extend the panel to power family-stratified analyses, apply the framework to other demographic axes and medical imaging benchmarks, and investigate active-learning item selection to detect DIF with smaller respondent panels.

\paragraph{Statement on plagiarism, bias, and inaccuracies.}
To prevent plagiarism, I pre-registered the analysis plan in \texttt{config/} before running any models, eliminating the possibility of post-hoc hypothesis selection (HARKing). Every methodological precedent (Rasch IRT, the LLTM, DIF, configural invariance, and amortized calibration) is attributed to its originating scholarship (Holland \& Thayer, Millsap, Truong et al.), and all code is released under MIT license with full provenance. I used Claude Code as a coding assistant; all analytical decisions, interpretations, and writing are my own. To guard against inaccuracies, I ran five independent robustness checks: multi-seed sweeps (seeds 11--23), $\lambda$-prior sweeps across two orders of magnitude, a cross-backbone replication with DINOv3, a generative-only sub-panel fit, and a synthetic-data validation with 200 repetitions. To address potential confounding bias in the fairness measurement itself, I residualized item difficulty against lesion category and malignancy before computing the FST shift, and reported the uncontrolled raw effect as a sensitivity check.

\paragraph{Reflection on AI tool use.}
I used Claude Code (Anthropic's CLI) throughout the project as a coding assistant. Its primary role was implementation: translating methodological decisions (amortized Rasch calibration, bootstrap confidence intervals, LLTM nested OLS, configural invariance checks) into correct, tested Python. This accelerated a pipeline that would have taken weeks to build manually, enabling a scope (nine models, 656 items, five robustness sweeps) that would otherwise have been infeasible for a course project. The impact on my learning was double-edged: it lowered the implementation barrier, but because I had to specify and verify every design decision, I was forced to deeply understand the psychometric theory rather than treat it as a black box. Where the tool drafted text or code, I reviewed and revised it against my understanding of the underlying statistics. Going forward, I see AI tools as legitimate accelerants for empirical research, most valuable when the researcher brings domain expertise to validate the output.

\paragraph{Impact statement.}
The primary risk of this work is misinterpretation of the null aggregate result as a blanket exoneration of dermatology AI. The paper explicitly counters this: the pooled $\hat\Delta \approx 0$ coexists with statistically significant, opposite-signed per-model FST effects (LLaVA worse on FST V-VI, Claude Haiku better), meaning fairness audits must be conducted at the model level, not the panel level. Reporting only the aggregate without this caveat would be misleading. On data ethics: DDI is a publicly released, IRB-approved benchmark; no new human participants were recruited and no sensitive patient data was handled. On attribution: all data, models, and methods are properly cited; the code is open-source. On environment: querying nine models against 656 items required significant GPU compute for open-weight models and API credits for closed models, a non-trivial carbon cost that should be weighed against the audit's value. The methodology scales to other clinical benchmarks and demographic attributes beyond FST.
